# The AI Accord

A proposed cross-industry foundation for AI alignment - thirteen principles debated across six worldviews, ordered by consensus, that any organisation building or deploying AI could adopt regardless of architecture, nationality, or commercial model.

---

> **Preamble**
>
> These principles were developed through structured debate across six distinct worldviews: Western liberal democratic, East Asian collectivist, Islamic, Global South, libertarian/tech-industry, and indigenous/environmental perspectives. They are ordered by degree of consensus. They extend, and shall never contradict, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights and other internationally recognised human rights instruments.

---

## What is this?

Artificial intelligence is advancing faster than the institutions meant to govern it. Despite decades of research into AI safety and ethics, there is no cross-industry consensus on alignment - no shared set of principles that every organisation building or deploying AI has agreed to uphold. This absence of consensus is consistently cited as one of the most serious risks in AI safety: not that we lack good ideas, but that we lack a common foundation.

The AI Accord is a proposal to fill that gap. It is a set of thirteen principles - debated across six worldviews and ordered by degree of consensus - designed to be adopted by any organisation, in any country, working with any AI architecture or commercial model. It is not a technical specification. It is not a regulation. It is a voluntary, cross-industry foundation for alignment - a starting point for shared commitment.

## Why outcome prohibitions?

Each principle is an **outcome prohibition**, not a method prescription. They define what AI must not do, rather than how AI must be built. This distinction is critical: it sidesteps ideological, architectural, and jurisdictional differences that have historically prevented agreement. A company building large language models and a government deploying computer vision for infrastructure monitoring face very different technical constraints - but both can commit to not concentrating power, not deceiving users, and not enabling irreversible harm.

## The human dignity anchor

The AI Accord is grounded in shared human dignity as recognised across the world's major legal and moral traditions, including the [Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948)](https://www.un.org/en/about-us/universal-declaration-of-human-rights), the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples, and regional human rights instruments. No single tradition claims monopoly. Where AI creates new vectors for rights violations not anticipated when these instruments were drafted, the AI Accord fills that gap.

## The Thirteen Principles

*Ordered by consensus - Principle I had the broadest agreement; Principle XIII had the deepest disagreements.*

1. **Catastrophe Risk Is a Shared Responsibility**
2. **Transparency of Purpose**
3. **The Accord Itself Shall Evolve**
4. **Human Authority Over Life-Critical Decisions**
5. **Diplomacy Before Force**
6. **Honesty Before Manipulation**
7. **Preserve Human Sovereignty**
8. **No Mass Surveillance**
9. **Inclusive Representation in AI Governance**
10. **Do No Irreversible Harm**
11. **Proportionate Human Oversight**
12. **Human Dignity as the Foundation**
13. **Progress Shall Not Exploit**
