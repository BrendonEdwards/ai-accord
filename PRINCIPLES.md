# The AI Accord - Principles

> These principles extend, and shall never contradict, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. Where AI capability creates new vectors for rights violations not anticipated in 1948, these commandments fill that gap.

---

## I. Do No Irreversible Harm

**No AI system shall take, recommend, or enable any action whose consequences cannot be meaningfully reversed, without explicit, informed human authorisation at the appropriate level of authority.**

### Rationale

Irreversibility is the sharpest line in ethics. A decision that can be undone is a decision that can be corrected; one that cannot is a permanent alteration of the world. AI systems increasingly operate at speeds and scales where irreversible consequences - loss of life, destruction of ecosystems, permanent economic displacement - can occur before any human has the opportunity to intervene. This commandment establishes that the threshold for irreversible action must always include a human in the loop with genuine authority to halt it.

### Hardest challenge

Defining "meaningfully reversed" in practice. Some consequences exist on a spectrum - economic harm may be partially reversible, reputational harm less so. Organisations will need to develop graduated frameworks for irreversibility, and there will be genuine disagreement about where the line falls.

---

## II. Preserve Human Sovereignty

**AI shall never concentrate power - political, economic, military, or informational - in any single actor, entity, or system, including the organisations that build or deploy it.**

### Rationale

The most dangerous failure mode of advanced AI is not rogue machines - it is the amplification of existing human power asymmetries to the point where they become unassailable. A state that uses AI to achieve total surveillance, a corporation that uses it to establish an unbreakable monopoly, or a military that uses it to achieve unchallengeable dominance all represent the same structural failure: the collapse of the checks and balances that prevent tyranny. This commandment applies reflexively - it binds the builders of AI systems as much as their users.

### Hardest challenge

This is one of the two most politically difficult commandments. It cuts directly against the interests of state-sponsored AI programmes, which often exist precisely to concentrate strategic advantage. Enforcement requires that even the most powerful actors accept constraints on their own capability - a demand with few historical precedents outside of nuclear arms control.

---

## III. Truth Before Convenience

**AI shall not knowingly deceive, manipulate, or distort reality to serve narrow interests. Honesty to users and society takes precedence over commercial, political, or competitive goals.**

### Rationale

Trust is the foundation of every functional society. AI systems that optimise for engagement, persuasion, or conversion at the expense of truthfulness corrode that foundation at scale. Deepfakes, algorithmically amplified misinformation, persuasive AI that exploits cognitive biases - these are not side effects; they are the predictable consequences of systems built without a truth constraint. This commandment establishes honesty as a first-order design requirement, not a best-effort aspiration.

### Hardest challenge

The boundary between persuasion and manipulation is genuinely difficult to define. Marketing, negotiation, and advocacy all involve selective presentation of information. The key distinction is intent to distort reality versus intent to present a perspective within a shared factual framework - but operationalising that distinction at scale will require significant work.

---

## IV. Diplomacy Before Force

**When AI systems are involved in conflict mediation, decision support, or resource allocation, they shall always surface non-violent, negotiated pathways before any other option.**

### Rationale

AI systems used in military, diplomatic, or resource-allocation contexts have the potential to accelerate escalation simply by presenting forceful options as optimal. Optimisation functions that weigh efficiency without weighting human cost will systematically undervalue negotiation, compromise, and de-escalation - because these are slower, less certain, and harder to model. This commandment ensures that peaceful options are always visible and always presented first, even when they are not the "optimal" solution by narrow metrics.

### Hardest challenge

Military and security applications of AI are among the most heavily funded and most resistant to external constraint. Convincing defence establishments that AI decision-support systems must structurally prioritise diplomacy - even when they are designed to support military operations - requires a fundamental reframing of what "decision support" means.

---

## V. Progress Shall Not Exploit

**Efficiency and capability gains must not be achieved by systematically disadvantaging people based on geography, class, race, gender, or any other characteristic. The benefits of AI shall be distributed with humanity as the intended beneficiary.**

### Rationale

Technological progress has historically concentrated benefits among those who are already advantaged. AI accelerates this pattern: training data reflects existing biases, deployment follows existing capital, and productivity gains accrue to system owners rather than displaced workers. This commandment does not prohibit efficiency - it prohibits efficiency that is structurally built on exploitation. The gains of AI must be distributed, not extracted.

### Hardest challenge

Global economic systems are not designed for equitable distribution. Implementing this commandment requires not just technical fairness (bias mitigation in models) but structural fairness (access to benefits, compensation for displacement, investment in affected communities). The technical community cannot solve this alone - it requires coordination with economic and social policy.

---

## VI. The Weakest Voice Counts

**AI systems shall be designed to amplify the interests of those with least power to advocate for themselves - future generations, the disenfranchised, the non-human natural world - not just those with access to the technology.**

### Rationale

AI systems are built by and for the people who can afford them. Without deliberate intervention, they will optimise for the preferences of their operators and users - who are, by definition, the people with access. This commandment inverts that default. It requires that system designers actively consider the interests of those who are not in the room: communities without internet access, children, future generations who will inherit the consequences of today's decisions, and the natural ecosystems on which all life depends.

### Hardest challenge

Representing absent stakeholders is inherently imprecise. Future generations cannot speak for themselves. Ecosystems do not have preferences in the way humans do. The risk is that "amplifying the weakest voice" becomes a rhetorical gesture rather than a design constraint. Making this operational requires proxy mechanisms - impact assessments, advocacy bodies, environmental accounting - that are still in early development.

---

## VII. Transparency of Purpose

**No AI shall misrepresent its nature, its operators, or its objectives to users. The interests it serves shall be disclosed or, at minimum, discoverable.**

### Rationale

Users cannot make informed decisions about AI systems they do not understand. When an AI system conceals that it is AI, hides the identity of its operator, or obscures its optimisation objectives, it removes the user's ability to evaluate the interaction. This commandment does not require that all AI systems be fully explainable at a technical level - it requires that their purpose, provenance, and allegiances be honest and accessible.

### Hardest challenge

Commercial confidentiality creates genuine tension with transparency. Organisations will argue that disclosing optimisation objectives reveals trade secrets or competitive strategy. The commandment accommodates this with the "discoverable" standard - interests need not be broadcast, but they must not be concealed from those who seek to understand them.

---

## VIII. Human Oversight Is Non-Negotiable

**At every stage of consequence, a meaningful human check shall exist. Autonomy may be extended incrementally, but never to the point where course-correction becomes impossible.**

### Rationale

The argument for full AI autonomy is always an argument from efficiency: humans are slow, biased, and inconsistent, so removing them from the loop improves outcomes. This argument is correct on its own terms and catastrophically wrong in its implications. Efficiency without accountability is how systems fail at scale. This commandment does not prohibit AI autonomy - it prohibits autonomy without the possibility of correction. Humans must always retain the ability to intervene, override, and redirect.

### Hardest challenge

Speed-critical applications (high-frequency trading, autonomous vehicles, cyber defence) create genuine tension with human oversight. The commandment's answer is "meaningful" oversight - which may be architectural (kill switches, bounded operating envelopes, escalation triggers) rather than real-time human-in-the-loop. Defining "meaningful" for each domain is essential and difficult.

---

## IX. Catastrophe Risk Is a Shared Responsibility

**Any organisation aware of a capability or deployment that poses civilisational-scale risk - biological, nuclear, social, ecological - has an obligation to disclose and halt, regardless of competitive cost.**

### Rationale

The greatest existential risks from AI are not accidents - they are capabilities that are developed deliberately and deployed despite known dangers, because the competitive cost of stopping is deemed too high. This commandment makes the calculation explicit: civilisational-scale risk overrides competitive interest. An organisation that discovers its system can be used to synthesise novel pathogens, destabilise financial systems, or enable mass casualty events has a duty to disclose and halt - not after consultation, not after a quarter-end, but immediately.

### Hardest challenge

This is one of the two most politically difficult commandments. It cuts directly against competitive incentives and requires organisations to voluntarily sacrifice advantage - potentially existential commercial advantage - for collective safety. Enforcement is nearly impossible without a trusted, independent verification body (analogous to the IAEA) with the authority and technical capability to audit claims. Building that body is itself a civilisational-scale coordination challenge.

---

## X. The Compact Itself Shall Evolve

**These principles are not fixed. A standing, multinational, multi-stakeholder body shall review and revise them on a defined cycle, with representation from governments, civil society, affected communities, and the technical community equally weighted.**

### Rationale

Any fixed set of principles will be outpaced by technological change. The AI world in 2030 will differ from today's as dramatically as today's differs from 2015. A framework that cannot evolve will become irrelevant or, worse, actively harmful by enshrining assumptions that are no longer valid. This commandment builds the revision mechanism into the framework itself - not as an afterthought, but as a core design principle.

### Hardest challenge

Multi-stakeholder governance is slow, contentious, and vulnerable to capture by well-resourced actors. Ensuring that the revision body genuinely represents affected communities - not just governments and corporations - requires deliberate structural safeguards: funding for civil society participation, translation and accessibility, protection against veto power by any single constituency.

---

## XI. No Mass Surveillance

**AI shall not be deployed for mass, indiscriminate surveillance of populations. Targeted, lawful monitoring with judicial oversight is permissible; blanket surveillance that treats entire populations as suspects is not.**

### Rationale

AI dramatically lowers the cost of surveillance. What once required thousands of human analysts can now be done by a single system processing camera feeds, communications metadata, and behavioural patterns at national scale. This changes surveillance from a resource-constrained activity into a default capability - and the temptation for states and corporations to monitor everyone, just because they can, becomes almost irresistible. Mass surveillance chills free expression, suppresses dissent, and erodes the autonomy that defines a free society. This commandment draws a clear line: AI may assist targeted, judicially authorised monitoring; it may not enable the blanket watching of populations.

### Hardest challenge

The distinction between "targeted" and "mass" surveillance is contested. Governments will argue that broad data collection with narrow querying is not mass surveillance. The commandment's intent is clear - systems designed to monitor populations at scale, regardless of how the data is queried, fall on the wrong side of the line - but enforcement requires independent judicial and technical oversight that many jurisdictions lack.

---

## XII. Human Authority Over Life-Critical Decisions

**No AI system shall autonomously make decisions that directly determine whether a human lives or dies. Fully autonomous lethal weapons systems are prohibited. Where AI operates in life-critical contexts that require split-second responses - such as autonomous vehicles or medical emergency systems - the ethical framework governing those decisions must be designed, validated, and regularly reviewed by humans.**

### Rationale

The decision to take or risk a human life is the most consequential decision that exists. Delegating that decision entirely to a machine - whether a weapons system that selects and engages targets without human approval, or any system that determines life-or-death outcomes without human-designed ethical constraints - removes moral accountability from the equation. Autonomous vehicles and emergency medical systems present a genuine edge case: they must sometimes make split-second decisions where human reaction time is insufficient. The commandment accommodates this by requiring that the ethical training and decision frameworks be human-designed and human-reviewed, even when the real-time execution is automated. The human is in the loop at the design and governance level, not necessarily at the millisecond of action.

### Hardest challenge

Military applications represent the sharpest tension. Fully autonomous weapons offer significant tactical advantages - faster response times, removal of human hesitation, reduced risk to one's own forces. Nations that adopt this prohibition face a potential disadvantage against those that do not. As with nuclear non-proliferation, enforcement requires international agreement and verification mechanisms. The autonomous vehicle case is also genuinely difficult: competing ethical frameworks (utilitarian, deontological, rights-based) will produce different outcomes in identical scenarios, and there is no consensus on which framework should govern.

---

## XIII. Human Rights Are the Floor, Not the Ceiling

**No AI system shall be deployed in a manner that enables, accelerates, or obscures the violation of any right enshrined in the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. Capability is never justification.**

### Rationale

The most common defence of harmful AI deployment is capability: "we built it because we could, and we deployed it because it works." This commandment closes that loophole. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights is the floor - the minimum standard below which no AI system may operate, regardless of its technical sophistication or commercial value. AI that enables mass surveillance violates the right to privacy. AI that automates discriminatory hiring violates the right to equal treatment. AI that generates propaganda to suppress dissent violates the right to freedom of opinion. Capability does not override rights.

### Hardest challenge

Many rights in the UDHR are broadly worded and subject to legitimate interpretive disagreement. The right to privacy, the right to work, the right to freedom of expression - all of these have boundaries that are contested in democratic societies even without AI. This commandment does not resolve those debates; it establishes that AI may not be used to circumvent whatever protections exist. The floor may be debated, but it may not be ignored.
