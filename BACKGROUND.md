# Background - The Case for the AI Accord

## The Current State of AI Alignment

There is no cross-industry consensus on AI alignment. This is not for lack of effort - hundreds of organisations have published ethics guidelines, safety frameworks, and responsible-AI principles. The problem is that these efforts are fragmented, voluntary, and structurally incapable of constraining the actors most likely to cause harm.

Corporate ethics statements are written by the companies they are meant to constrain. National AI strategies serve national interests. Academic frameworks lack enforcement mechanisms. Multi-stakeholder initiatives (the OECD AI Principles, the G7 Hiroshima Process, the EU AI Act) represent important progress, but they are either non-binding, jurisdictionally limited, or focused on regulatory compliance rather than foundational alignment.

The result is a situation where "alignment" means different things to different actors, and where the organisations with the most advanced capabilities face the least external pressure to adopt shared constraints. This is widely cited as one of the most serious risks in AI safety - not because individual actors are malicious, but because the absence of a shared framework creates a race dynamic where safety is a competitive cost rather than a universal requirement.

## Why This Is Dangerous

Without a cross-industry consensus:

- **Safety becomes a competitive disadvantage.** Organisations that invest in alignment bear costs that their less cautious competitors do not. Over time, this selects for less cautious actors at the frontier.
- **Regulatory arbitrage flourishes.** Companies can develop capabilities in jurisdictions with minimal oversight and deploy them globally. No single regulator can constrain a globally distributed technology.
- **Public trust erodes.** When every company has its own definition of "responsible AI," the term becomes meaningless. Public scepticism grows, and with it the risk of either under-regulation (because governance is too slow) or over-regulation (because fear fills the vacuum left by trust).
- **Existential risks go unmanaged.** The most dangerous capabilities - those that could pose civilisational-scale risks - are precisely the capabilities that competitive incentives discourage disclosing. Without a shared obligation to disclose and halt, the default is silence.

## Historical Precedents

Humanity has faced civilisational-scale risks before and has, imperfectly but meaningfully, managed them through international agreement. Three precedents are particularly instructive.

### The Geneva Conventions (1864–1949)

The Geneva Conventions established that even in war - the domain of maximum state interest - there are limits on permissible conduct. They succeeded not because they eliminated violations, but because they created a shared normative framework that made violations identifiable, nameable, and prosecutable. Before the Conventions, the treatment of prisoners and civilians in wartime was a matter of custom and convenience. After them, it was a matter of law.

The AI Accord draws on this precedent: the goal is not to eliminate harmful AI, but to establish a shared framework that makes harmful AI identifiable and indefensible.

### The Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (1968)

The NPT is the primary example of a framework that manages a technology whose uncontrolled spread poses existential risk. It is deeply imperfect - it enshrines an asymmetry between nuclear-weapon states and non-nuclear-weapon states, and it has not prevented proliferation entirely. But it has dramatically slowed the spread of nuclear weapons and created a normative environment in which acquisition is costly and concealment is difficult.

The parallel to AI is direct: uncontrolled proliferation of advanced AI capabilities, without shared safety constraints, poses risks that no single actor can manage alone.

### The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA)

Of the three precedents, the IAEA is the most structurally relevant to AI governance. The IAEA is not just a treaty body - it is an operational organisation with the technical expertise to verify compliance, the authority to conduct inspections, and the independence to report findings without state approval.

An effective AI governance framework will eventually require something analogous: an international body with the technical capability to evaluate AI systems, the authority to audit claims of safety and compliance, and the independence to act on findings. This body does not yet exist, and building it is one of the most important coordination challenges of the coming decade.

The IAEA model is not perfect - it is constrained by the political dynamics of the UN Security Council, and its authority is limited to states that have accepted its jurisdiction. But it demonstrates that technically complex verification of dual-use technology is possible at international scale, and that an independent body can meaningfully constrain state behaviour even in domains of intense strategic competition.

## The Human Rights Anchor

The AI Accord is grounded in the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) for both strategic and moral reasons.

### Strategic importance

The UDHR is the most widely endorsed statement of shared human values in history. It has been ratified or endorsed by virtually every nation-state, and its principles - however imperfectly implemented - represent the closest thing humanity has to a universal moral baseline. Anchoring the AI Accord in the UDHR means anchoring it in a framework that already commands near-universal legitimacy. This is not a minor advantage. Any new framework for AI alignment must overcome the objection that it reflects the values of its authors rather than universal values. The UDHR provides the answer: these are not new values. They are existing values, applied to new capabilities.

### Moral importance

AI creates new vectors for rights violations that the drafters of the UDHR could not have anticipated:

- **Mass surveillance** at a scale and precision that makes the right to privacy (Article 12) unenforceable through traditional means.
- **Algorithmic discrimination** that violates the right to equal treatment (Article 7) through systems so complex that the discrimination is invisible to those affected.
- **Autonomous weapons** that challenge the right to life (Article 3) by removing human judgement from lethal decisions.
- **Synthetic media** - deepfakes, generated text, AI-driven propaganda - that undermines the right to freedom of opinion (Article 19) by making it impossible to distinguish truth from fabrication.

The UDHR was written for a world of human actors. AI extends the reach and speed of human action by orders of magnitude. The rights enshrined in 1948 are not wrong - they are incomplete. The AI Accord fills the gaps.

## The Two Hardest Commandments

Two commandments stand out as the most politically difficult to implement.

### Commandment II: Preserve Human Sovereignty

*AI shall never concentrate power - political, economic, military, or informational - in any single actor, entity, or system, including the organisations that build or deploy it.*

This commandment cuts directly against the interests of state-sponsored AI programmes, which often exist precisely to achieve strategic advantage - that is, to concentrate power. China's national AI strategy, the United States' investment in military AI, and similar programmes in Russia, Israel, the UK, and elsewhere are all predicated on the assumption that AI leadership confers decisive advantage.

Commandment II says that decisive advantage is precisely the outcome to be prevented. It applies to states, to corporations, and to the AI systems themselves. This makes it politically explosive - and indispensable. History shows that unchecked concentration of power, by any actor, is the single most reliable precursor to systemic human rights violations.

### Commandment IX: Catastrophe Risk Is a Shared Responsibility

*Any organisation aware of a capability or deployment that poses civilisational-scale risk has an obligation to disclose and halt, regardless of competitive cost.*

This commandment cuts directly against competitive incentives. It requires organisations to voluntarily sacrifice advantage - potentially existential commercial advantage - for collective safety. In a market economy, this is an extraordinary demand. It requires that the incentive to survive as a company be subordinated to the obligation to protect civilisation.

Enforcement of Commandment IX is nearly impossible without a trusted, independent verification body - the IAEA analogue described above. Without such a body, the commandment relies on voluntary compliance, whistleblower protections, and the normative pressure created by the framework itself. These are not sufficient in the long term, but they are a necessary starting point.

## The Design Principle: Outcome Prohibitions, Not Method Prescriptions

The AI Accord is structured as a set of outcome prohibitions rather than method prescriptions. This is the key design principle that makes it agreeable across ideological, architectural, and national lines.

**Method prescriptions** specify how systems must be built: "use interpretable models," "implement RLHF," "require human-in-the-loop for all decisions." These fracture immediately along technical and ideological lines. What counts as "interpretable" depends on your field. Whether RLHF is sufficient depends on your threat model. Whether human-in-the-loop is feasible depends on your application domain.

**Outcome prohibitions** specify what must not happen: "do not concentrate power," "do not deceive," "do not enable irreversible harm without human authorisation." These are compatible with any architecture, any methodology, and any national regulatory framework. You can build your system however you want - but it must not produce these outcomes.

This distinction is what makes the AI Accord viable as a cross-industry, cross-national framework. It does not require agreement on how to build safe AI. It requires agreement on what safe AI must not do. And that agreement, while difficult, is far more achievable than consensus on methods.

The commandment format reinforces this design. Commandments are absolute within their domain - they do not say "try to avoid harm" or "consider the interests of the weakest." They say "do not" and "shall." This clarity is intentional. A framework built on aspirations is a framework that can be optimised around. A framework built on prohibitions creates a floor that cannot be negotiated away.
